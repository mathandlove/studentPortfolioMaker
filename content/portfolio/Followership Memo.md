# Followership Interview

**To:** Professor Reed and ENLP 3100 Class
**From:** Jordan Miller, ENLP Student
**Date:** 9/16/2025
**Subject:** Followership Interview

This past Friday, I conducted an interview that helped bring insight into my perspectives regarding what it means to be a good follower. The interview was interesting to me because it focused on a general theme: that effective followers must have the initiative to lead themselves. Before this interview, I had not considered how closely related followership and leadership really are. However, after this conversation, my initial perspectives have been challenged. I now understand that the role of followership is far more nuanced than I initially expected.

## Interview Background

Last Friday, I interviewed one of my closest mentors, David Carson. I met Mr. Carson through my time in scouting; however, he has advised me in various aspects of my life, especially with my decision to attend the university. He was my Scoutmaster when I served as Senior Patrol Leader of my Scout troop, he was my Eagle Project Mentor, and he was the single most influential person throughout my scouting career. Mr. Carson currently works as head of research at a research organization; however, he also has experience in journalism and teaches at a local university. Our interview lasted about 40 minutes and was performed over the phone at 8 AM.

## Key Takeaways

The interview began with defining Mr. Carson's opinion on what it means to be a good follower, then it shifted to how followership is understood through the perspective of scouting. The key takeaways of our conversation are as follows:

- The follower's job is to support the goals and objectives of the team.
- The follower has a responsibility to build up the leader.
- The follower also has a responsibility to lead themselves.
- Good leaders delegate, and good followers execute.
- Good leaders are good communicators, and good followers are good listeners.
- Good followers evaluate whether a leader's decision is beneficial for the team's goals.
- A good follower has to lead themselves to get the job done.
- Successful leaders cultivate talent in their followers.
- The leadership model of scouting is more hierarchical than most environments.
- In scouting, we practice "Service Followership."
- I served as an effective follower as the Troop Instructor of my Scout troop.
- To be a good follower in my career, I should be active and take initiative when I am delegated responsibility. The expectation is that I lead myself to get the job done.

## Analysis

It is clear from my interview with Mr. Carson that he believes followers have a responsibility to lead themselves. He was very clear about his understanding of the relationship between leaders and followers. The leader's job is to make decisions that benefit the team, evaluate how to leverage the followers' skills and abilities, and delegate responsibility. The follower's job is to listen effectively, consider whether the leader's decision is in the interest of the team's success, understand the responsibility that has been delegated to them, and lead themselves to execute that responsibility. According to Mr. Carson, the success of the team is far more dependent on having good followers than having a good leader. While both roles are important for team success, he reasons that a typical team is comprised mostly of followers. If those followers have initiative and are goal-oriented, the team can still find success with a weak leader. However, the reverse is not always true. If a team has followers who lack initiative and misunderstand their role in supporting the team's progress, a good leader will have far less impact on the success of the team. Ultimately, good followership is at the heart of team success. This was a new perspective for me. I had always thought that the success of the team was mostly influenced by the effectiveness of the leader. This change in perspective was interesting to me because it totally changes how I think about constructing strong teams. A strong team is comprised of individuals who can be delegated responsibility and execute it themselves. As a leader myself, this perspective will affect how I approach leading others. Instead of micromanaging my team, it is my responsibility to communicate and enable them. Then it is important for me to give them the freedom to execute their task.

Followership in scouting is similar to followership in other environments, like school and work; however, there is one key difference. Scouting has a clear hierarchy of leadership that resembles the military. As such, there is a clear difference between leadership and those who follow. My interview with Mr. Carson highlighted this difference by discussing how the role of followership changes in the workplace. Mr. Carson brought to my attention that in the workplace, it may not always be clear who the "leader" is. He noted that in these cases, an individual may be filling the roles of both a leader and a follower. I found this point interesting because it made me reflect on the times I have had to step up as a leader when I was also serving as a follower. It seems that more often than not, followers will be asked to step up as leaders in some capacity to execute their task. This realization made me recognize that to be a better follower, it is important to further develop my personal leadership skills.

Ultimately, this interview was incredibly informative. It challenged my initial perspectives regarding followership and helped me realize how closely related leadership and followership are.
