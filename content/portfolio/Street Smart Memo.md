> "The textbooks only take you so far."

Today, I conducted an interview that pushed my understanding of what it means to be street smart. I entered the conversation having some assumptions about how my interviewee uses street smarts in their career, but by the end of our conversation, I had a much deeper understanding of why street smarts and experience are so critical in their line of work.

## Interview Background

Today I interviewed my father, Robert Miller. Mr. Miller is a Firefighter and Paramedic for the city fire department. He has been working as a firefighter for over 20 years and is currently a Lieutenant on the Technical Rescue Team at his station. When I learned that we were conducting an interview about "Street Smarts," I immediately thought of my Dad. He is by far the most street-smart individual that I know, and I knew his perspective on this topic would be incredibly interesting. He regularly leverages his street-smart experience as a senior crew member and leader in the firehouse. This is why I got specific permission to interview him for this assignment. Ultimately, our conversation exceeded expectations. We had our interview this afternoon in person, and it lasted about 30 minutes.

## Key Takeaways

We began the interview by identifying the difference between book smarts and street smarts in the fire service. Then we took a deep look into how experience and street knowledge make a meaningful difference in the way Mr. Miller leads his crew. The key takeaways of our conversation are as follows:

- There are two types of book-smart knowledge in the fire service:
    - Emergency Response (studying fires, building construction, rescue strategy, etc.)
    - Emergency Medicine (paramedicine, cardiac events, administering drugs, etc.)
- Book smart knowledge is taught in the fire academy and in paramedic school.
- Firefighting street smarts are location-specific (one city vs. another).
- Street smart knowledge makes a huge difference in personal and crew safety.
- The majority of being a firefighter is learned through shadowing other firefighters.
- New guys learn skills and strategies by observing more experienced guys.
- Stories told at the dinner table help teach the lessons that can't be shown in person.
- Street smarts affect how technical tasks are completed (the academy doesn't always teach the best or most efficient technique).
- Street smarts affect how the crew interacts with patients and the community.
- Street smarts affect how you are respected/accepted within the crew.

## Analysis

Mr. Miller is one of the most experienced individuals in his firehouse. As a senior guy who has opted to remain in the rank of Lieutenant, he is in a unique position to lead his crew in the field. Traditionally, in the fire service, senior personnel continue to rank up and leave the firehouse. After the rank of Captain (one rank above Lieutenant), a senior member rarely interacts with the new crews. Since Mr. Miller has intentionally remained as a Lieutenant, he preserves his ability to lead and share his experience. With experience comes street smarts. The most interesting part of my conversation with Mr. Miller was discussing how street smarts affect the ways his crew interacts with patients and the public. Most specifically, understanding a patient's culture changes how they approach emergency response and emergency medicine. Having worked in the same area for so long has allowed him to gain a rich understanding of the local cultures. We discussed a specific example when understanding community culture affects procedure regarding trauma. Traditionally, in a trauma response situation, the book teaches paramedics to "strip" the patient completely naked to inspect them for injury. However, in certain cultures present in his community - especially when interacting with women - that type of procedure can be opposed to religious beliefs. Paramedics are not taught to consider cultural norms in trauma situations; however, Mr. Miller has learned through years of experience that if the situation does not warrant a "strip," it is safer for the patient and for his crew to go against procedure. This example was interesting to me because if I were in the shoes of a brand-new paramedic, I would be uncomfortable going against procedure, especially when the implications could affect the patient's health. During our conversation, we discussed multiple other examples of how cultural norms dictate their crews' interactions with patients and the public. Arguably, the most interesting application of the street smarts was how Mr. Miller interacts with the public to ensure his crew's safety and the safety of the public. Our conversation was incredibly insightful and has led me to question how working in different cultural environments dictates how emergency responders approach emergency care. This inquiry has also prompted me to consider how cultural norms will impact my personal leadership style for the future.
