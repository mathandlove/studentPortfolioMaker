# Family Intelligence Interview

**To:** Professor Reed and ENLP 3100 Class
**From:** Jordan Miller, ENLP Student
**Date:** 8/28/2025
**Subject:** Family Intelligence Interview

Today, I conducted an interview that provided deep insight into the factors that shape my personal opinions on intelligence. The interview was very revealing and interesting because it highlighted potential explanations for the way I think about intelligence and why being intelligent is important to me. While I predicted some answers to the questions asked, certain questions revealed unexpected information and have sparked a deeper inquiry into this subject.

## Interview Background

This afternoon, I interviewed my mother, Diane Miller. Mrs. Miller has been one of the most influential people in my life, not only because she is my mother, but because she has consistently set an outstanding example for me to look up to since I was little. She currently works on a cutting-edge research project at a major pharmaceutical company, dedicated to curing kids with leukemia. The interview lasted about 20 minutes and was performed over the phone around 5 PM.

## Key Takeaways

The interview began by defining Mrs. Miller's understanding of what it means to be intelligent, then it shifted and explored how our family values intelligence in general. The key takeaways of the conversation are as follows:

- Academic performance is traditionally society's standard for intelligence, especially for people my age.
- In our family, academic performance is not the best way to measure intelligence.
- Academic performance is a better measure of effort than intelligence.
- Intelligent people have an enhanced ability to learn new things.
- My family values consistently learning new things.
- In my family, emotional intelligence is incredibly important. We value emotional intelligence higher than academic intelligence.
- As a family of leaders, we recognize that "intelligent leadership is not about knowing everything. It's about allowing the people who are more knowledgeable than me to do their job."
- Intelligent leadership starts with caring about the people we lead.
- Our family recognizes when its members achieve great things. We celebrate each other for our hard work and application of our intelligence.
- I align very closely with my family's definition of intelligence.

## Analysis

It is clear from my interview with Mrs. Miller that my family has a deeply nuanced understanding of what it means to be an intelligent person. Most importantly, we value the ability to interact with others, "read the room," and have empathy for those around us. There is a deep connection between intelligence and effective leadership in my family. As leaders in their respective fields, my parents take pride in building relationships with their teams and taking the necessary steps to interact with them on an emotional level. As a result, emotional intelligence is highly valued in my family. During this conversation, I learned more about the role of emotional intelligence in my father's job (he is a firefighter). I initially thought the fire service was a fairly emotionally disconnected line of work. However, after discussing with my mother, I realized how critical it is for my dad to be emotionally invested, especially in the crew he leads. My family also takes pride in regularly learning new things. We challenge ourselves by taking risks and putting ourselves out there. During this conversation, I learned about another one of my mother's new endeavors. She is currently learning how to weld - a completely new skill for her and something she has been wanting to try for a while. These qualities are important to our family and highlight what we truly believe makes us intelligent. We take the time to lift one another up and recognize each other when we apply ourselves. The emotional intelligence within my family is revealed when we take the time to celebrate each other. This interview was incredibly interesting to me because my family and I are so closely aligned in our understanding of what it means to be intelligent. In my autoethnographic reflection, I stressed the importance of social and flexible intelligence - each of these ideas was brought up unprompted by my mother during the interview. It made me proud to realize how aligned I am with my family on this subject.
